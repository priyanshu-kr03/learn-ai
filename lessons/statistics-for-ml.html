Mean median mode

Detailed explanation

Mean (arithmetic mean) is the sum of all values divided by the number of values: μ = Σx_i / n. It is sensitive to outliers — a single extreme value shifts the mean significantly. Mean is appropriate for symmetric distributions without outliers. In ML: the sample mean estimates the population mean and is used in standardization (subtract mean, divide by std).

Median is the middle value when all values are sorted. For an odd number of values, it is the exact middle. For an even number, it is the average of the two middle values. The median is robust to outliers — the median of [1, 2, 3, 4, 100] is 3, regardless of the outlier 100. Use the median for skewed data (income, price, house value) and when outliers should not influence the central tendency estimate.

Mode is the most frequently occurring value. For a discrete distribution: the category with the highest count. For a continuous distribution: the peak of the PDF. Mode is the only central tendency measure applicable to purely categorical data. In ML: sklearn's SimpleImputer with strategy='most_frequent' imputes missing categorical values with the mode.

When to use each: for a normal distribution, mean = median = mode. For right-skewed data (income), mean > median > mode — the mean is 'pulled up' by the long tail. Report the median for skewed distributions in EDA. For categorical data, mode is the only option. For model performance metrics, mean is appropriate (mean accuracy across CV folds).

Weighted mean assigns different importance to each value: weighted_mean = Σ(w_i * x_i) / Σw_i. In ML: class-weighted evaluation (give more weight to minority class performance), model ensemble (weighted average of model predictions), and feature importance aggregation (weighted by sample frequency).

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

import numpy as np; from scipy import stats
data = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 100]  # outlier present
print(f'Mean:   {np.mean(data):.2f}')    # 19.17 — pulled up by outlier
print(f'Median: {np.median(data):.2f}')  # 3.50 — robust
print(f'Mode:   {stats.mode(data).mode[0]:.2f}')  # 1 (first unique element)
Income: income = np.random.lognormal(10, 1, 1000)
print(f'Mean income: ${np.mean(income):,.0f}')     # high, skewed up by outliers
print(f'Median income: ${np.median(income):,.0f}')  # more representative
Imputation: from sklearn.impute import SimpleImputer
num_imputer = SimpleImputer(strategy='mean')    # or 'median' for skewed features
cat_imputer = SimpleImputer(strategy='most_frequent')
Why this matters in AI
Mean, median, and mode are the foundational descriptive statistics. Choosing the wrong central tendency measure misleads analysis: reporting the mean income in a skewed distribution makes 'average' income seem higher than most people earn. In ML, using mean vs median imputation for missing values significantly impacts model performance on skewed features.
Common mistakes
  • Using mean for skewed distributions — the mean income misrepresents typical income when a few billionaires skew the distribution. Always check skewness before choosing mean or median.
  • Reporting only the mean without spread — mean alone is insufficient. Report mean ± std, or median + IQR, to communicate both central tendency and variability.
  • Using mode imputation for numerical features — mode imputation for continuous data puts all missing values at one specific value, creating an artificial spike in the distribution. Use mean or median instead.

Variance standard deviation

Detailed explanation

Variance measures the spread of a distribution — how far values are from the mean on average. Population variance: σ² = Σ(x_i - μ)² / N. Sample variance: s² = Σ(x_i - x̄)² / (n-1). The (n-1) denominator (Bessel's correction) gives an unbiased estimate of population variance. Standard deviation (σ or s) is the square root of variance — it is in the same units as the original data, making it interpretable.

Low variance means values cluster tightly around the mean. High variance means values are spread widely. A feature with near-zero variance adds no information (all values are essentially the same). sklearn's VarianceThreshold removes features with variance below a threshold — a simple feature selection method.

Standard deviation in ML: (1) Z-score normalization: (x - mean) / std makes features have mean=0, std=1. (2) Model uncertainty: the std of predictions across multiple models (bootstrap ensemble) quantifies prediction uncertainty. (3) Learning rate schedules: reduce learning rate when the validation loss std over recent epochs is high (unstable training). (4) Data augmentation noise: adding Gaussian noise with std=0.1 to training images.

Standard error vs standard deviation: standard deviation describes the spread of individual measurements. Standard error of the mean (SEM = σ/sqrt(n)) describes the precision of the sample mean estimate. The SEM decreases as n increases — with more data, our estimate of the mean is more precise. Report SEM when discussing how precisely you know the mean; report SD when describing the spread of data.

Coefficient of variation (CV = σ/μ * 100%) is a dimensionless measure of relative variability. It allows comparing spread across features with different scales or units. CV of 5% means the std is 5% of the mean — relatively low variation. CV of 100% means the std equals the mean — high variation. Use CV to compare the variability of features before deciding which to engineer.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

data = np.array([2, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 7, 9])
print(f'Mean: {data.mean():.2f}, Std: {data.std():.2f}, Var: {data.var():.2f}')
Bessel's correction: print(data.std(ddof=0))  # population std (N)
print(data.std(ddof=1))  # sample std (N-1) — unbiased estimate
Variance threshold: from sklearn.feature_selection import VarianceThreshold
vt = VarianceThreshold(threshold=0.01)  # remove near-constant features
X_reduced = vt.fit_transform(X)
print(f'Features kept: {X_reduced.shape[1]} of {X.shape[1]}')
Prediction uncertainty: predictions = np.array([model.predict(X) for model in ensemble_models])
mean_pred = predictions.mean(axis=0); std_pred = predictions.std(axis=0)
uncertain = std_pred > 0.3  # flag high-uncertainty predictions
Why this matters in AI
Variance and standard deviation measure data spread — essential for understanding feature distributions, assessing model uncertainty, and making decisions about normalization and regularization. A model that reports only a mean prediction without uncertainty is dangerously overconfident.
Common mistakes
  • Confusing population and sample variance — for parameter estimation from data, always use sample variance (ddof=1). Population variance (ddof=0) underestimates true variance for samples.
  • Using variance for features on different scales — variance is scale-dependent. A feature with values in thousands has much higher variance than one in [0,1]. Use coefficient of variation for cross-feature comparison.
  • Ignoring variance in model evaluation — reporting mean F1 across CV folds without std gives an incomplete picture. A model with mean F1=0.82, std=0.12 is less reliable than one with mean=0.80, std=0.02.

Percentile quartile

Detailed explanation

Percentiles and quartiles are measures of position that describe where a value falls within a distribution. The p-th percentile is the value below which p% of observations fall. Quartiles are specific percentiles: Q1=25th percentile, Q2=50th percentile (median), Q3=75th percentile, Q4=100th percentile. These are essential for data exploration, outlier detection, and feature engineering.

IQR (Interquartile Range) = Q3 - Q1 — the range containing the middle 50% of data. The IQR is robust to outliers because it ignores the top and bottom 25%. Box plots visualize Q1, Q2, Q3, IQR, and outliers in one compact graphic. A box plot immediately reveals: median (central tendency), spread (IQR), skewness (is the median close to Q1 or Q3?), and outliers (points beyond Q3 + 1.5×IQR).

Percentile-based normalization (percentile rank): instead of standardizing with mean/std (sensitive to outliers), convert each value to its percentile rank: rank(x) / n. This gives a uniform distribution over [0, 1]. More robust than z-score normalization when outliers are present. sklearn's QuantileTransformer does this and can map to any output distribution (uniform or normal).

Quantile binning: convert a continuous variable into discrete bins based on quantile boundaries. pd.qcut(df['income'], q=4) creates 4 bins with approximately equal numbers of samples (quartiles). Unlike pd.cut (equal-width bins), pd.qcut ensures each bin has similar support. Useful when the predictor has a long tail and you want equal representation across bins.

Percentile in ML evaluation: some metrics use percentile reasoning. NDCG (ranking quality) considers the percentile rank of relevant items. In load testing, you track p50, p90, p95, p99 latency — not mean latency — because the mean hides that 1% of requests take 10x longer. For SLA compliance, you must meet the p99 target.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
# Percentile computation
data = np.random.lognormal(mean=4, sigma=1, size=10000)  # right-skewed distribution
percentiles = [10, 25, 50, 75, 90, 95, 99]
for p in percentiles:
    print(f'P{p:3d}: {np.percentile(data, p):8.2f}')
# Box plot stats
Q1, Q2, Q3 = np.percentile(data, [25, 50, 75])
IQR = Q3 - Q1
print(f'Q1={Q1:.2f}, Median={Q2:.2f}, Q3={Q3:.2f}, IQR={IQR:.2f}')
lower_fence, upper_fence = Q1 - 1.5 * IQR, Q3 + 1.5 * IQR
n_outliers = ((data < lower_fence) | (data > upper_fence)).sum()
print(f'Outliers (IQR method): {n_outliers} ({100*n_outliers/len(data):.1f}%)')
# Quantile binning
df_series = pd.Series(data)
quartile_bins = pd.qcut(df_series, q=4, labels=['Q1', 'Q2', 'Q3', 'Q4'])
print(quartile_bins.value_counts().sort_index())
Why this matters in AI
Percentiles are the correct descriptive statistics for skewed distributions. Salary data: mean salary can be $80K while median is $55K because a few million-dollar earners pull the mean up. Reporting the median gives a more representative picture. Response time SLAs should specify the 99th percentile ('p99 < 200ms') because mean latency hides tail behavior that determines user experience.
Common mistakes
  • Using mean to describe a skewed distribution — for right-skewed data (income, prices, response times), the mean is higher than what most values are. Use median for central tendency and IQR for spread when data is skewed.
  • Using pd.cut (equal width) when you want pd.qcut (equal frequency) — pd.cut with 4 bins splits by equal value ranges: $0-$25K, $25K-$50K, $50K-$75K, $75K+. If most salaries are $30K-$60K, the first and last bins will be nearly empty. pd.qcut creates bins with equal numbers of data points — each quartile contains exactly 25% of the data.
  • Confusing the p-th percentile with the p-th value — the 90th percentile of response time means 90% of requests are FASTER than this value, and 10% are SLOWER. 'P99 = 500ms' means the slowest 1% of requests take 500ms or more.

Covariance correlation

Detailed explanation

Covariance measures the direction of the linear relationship between two variables. cov(X, Y) = E[(X - μX)(Y - μY)] — the expected product of deviations from the mean. Positive covariance: X and Y tend to be above/below their means together (they move in the same direction). Negative covariance: when X is above its mean, Y tends to be below (they move in opposite directions). Zero covariance: no linear relationship.

Correlation: the dimensionless version of covariance. Pearson correlation: r = cov(X, Y) / (σX × σY). Range: [-1, +1]. r = +1: perfect positive linear relationship. r = -1: perfect negative linear relationship. r = 0: no linear relationship. Correlation allows comparison across variables with different units — income (dollars) and age (years) have a correlation of, say, 0.3, regardless of the different units.

Correlation matrix: a K×K symmetric matrix (K = number of features) where entry (i, j) is the correlation between feature i and feature j. Diagonal is always 1.0 (each feature perfectly correlates with itself). Used for: feature selection (find redundant features with high mutual correlation), multicollinearity detection (for linear models, highly correlated features cause unstable coefficients), understanding data structure.

Spearman rank correlation: a non-parametric alternative to Pearson that measures monotonic (not necessarily linear) relationships. Computes the correlation of ranks rather than raw values. More robust to outliers and non-linear monotonic relationships. Use Spearman when the relationship might be monotonic but not linear (e.g., correlation between income and happiness might be log-linear, not linear).

Point-biserial and Cramér's V: for correlation between a continuous feature and binary label, use point-biserial correlation (mathematically equivalent to Pearson for binary variables) or compare distributions (Mann-Whitney U test). For two categorical variables, use Cramér's V: V = sqrt(χ²/(n × min(K-1, L-1))). These extend correlation analysis to mixed feature types, which is essential in ML feature selection.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

import pandas as pd
import seaborn as sns

plt.figure(figsize=(12, 10))
sns.heatmap(corr_matrix, annot=True, fmt='.2f', cmap='RdBu_r', center=0, vmin=-1, vmax=1)
plt.title('Correlation Matrix')
# Feature-target correlation
corr_with_target = df.corr()['target'].sort_values(ascending=False)
print(corr_with_target.drop('target'))
# Spearman rank correlation
corr_spearman = df.corr(method='spearman')
# Point-biserial (feature vs binary target)
from scipy.stats import pointbiserialr
for col in df.select_dtypes(include='number').columns:
    r, p = pointbiserialr(df['binary_target'], df[col])
    print(f'{col}: r={r:.3f}, p={p:.4f}')
Why this matters in AI
Correlation analysis is the first step in feature selection and understanding data structure. Highly correlated features are redundant — keeping all of them wastes model capacity and causes instability in linear models. Low correlation with the target reveals irrelevant features. Correlation analysis guides feature engineering decisions.
Common mistakes
  • Interpreting zero correlation as no relationship — correlation measures LINEAR relationship. Two variables can have r=0 but a strong non-linear relationship (e.g., y = x², which has zero Pearson correlation with x but a perfect quadratic relationship).
  • Using correlation to imply causation — X and Y can be highly correlated because they are both caused by a third variable Z (confounding). Correlation is observational, not causal.
  • Using Pearson correlation when variables are non-normal or have outliers — one extreme outlier can dramatically change Pearson r. Use Spearman rank correlation for robustness.

Sampling

Detailed explanation

Sampling is the process of selecting a subset of individuals from a population to estimate properties of the whole. In statistics, we rarely have access to the entire population, so we draw samples. In ML, we sample mini-batches from the training dataset, we sample validation sets from labeled data, and we sample from probability distributions to generate data (Monte Carlo methods, generative models).

Random sampling (simple random sampling) gives every member of the population an equal chance of being selected. This is the gold standard — it minimizes selection bias. In Python: random.sample(population, k) without replacement; np.random.choice(population, size=k, replace=False). Stratified sampling ensures that subgroups (classes) are represented proportionally: sklearn's train_test_split(stratify=y) guarantees that class ratios are preserved in train and test splits.

Mini-batch sampling in deep learning: the training dataset is too large to fit in GPU memory at once. Instead, we iterate over randomly shuffled mini-batches. Each epoch randomly shuffles the dataset and splits it into batches of size B. The gradient estimate from a mini-batch is noisier than the true gradient (computed over all data) but is much faster to compute. Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) uses batch size 1; mini-batch SGD uses typical batch sizes of 32–512.

Bootstrap sampling: sample n examples with replacement from a dataset of n examples. Each bootstrap sample will contain approximately 63% unique examples (1 - (1-1/n)^n ≈ 1 - 1/e ≈ 0.632 for large n) and 37% duplicates. The remaining ~37% are 'out-of-bag' (OOB) examples. Bootstrap is used in: Random Forest (each tree trains on a bootstrap sample), confidence interval estimation, and the bootstrap statistical test.

Monte Carlo sampling: use random samples to estimate quantities that are analytically intractable. Monte Carlo integration: estimate π by sampling random points in a unit square and counting how many fall inside the unit circle. In ML: Monte Carlo dropout (sample multiple predictions with different dropout masks to estimate prediction uncertainty). MCMC (Markov Chain Monte Carlo) samples from complex posterior distributions in Bayesian inference.

Bootstrap CI: means = [np.mean(np.random.choice(data, size=len(data), replace=True)) for _ in range(1000)]

Why this matters in AI
Sampling is how ML deals with finite data and large datasets. Mini-batch training is the foundation of deep learning optimization. Stratified sampling prevents class imbalance in test sets. Understanding bootstrap enables constructing confidence intervals without normality assumptions.
Common mistakes
  • Not setting random seeds before sampling — results are irreproducible. Always set np.random.seed(42) or use random_state=42.
  • Using random split without stratification for imbalanced classification — a random 80/20 split on a 5% positive rate dataset may put only 2% positives in test by chance.
  • Sampling with replacement when you want without replacement — replace=True allows the same example to appear multiple times; replace=False ensures uniqueness.

Central Limit Theorem

Detailed explanation

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states: if X_1, X_2, ..., X_n are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables with mean μ and variance σ², then the sample mean X̄ = (X_1 + ... + X_n)/n converges in distribution to a normal distribution N(μ, σ²/n) as n → ∞, regardless of the original distribution's shape. This is one of the most powerful results in statistics.

Why the CLT matters: it justifies using the normal distribution (and its associated tests and confidence intervals) even when the underlying data is not normally distributed — as long as the sample size is large enough (typically n ≥ 30) and the samples are independent. This is why statistical tests like the t-test and z-test work in practice.

The CLT in ML: it justifies using the mean of cross-validation scores as a reliable performance estimate. The mean of 10 CV fold scores is approximately normally distributed (by CLT), regardless of the fold-to-fold variability distribution. This enables constructing confidence intervals around CV mean accuracy: CI = mean ± 1.96 * (std/sqrt(n_folds)).

The CLT explains why gradient descent works. The mini-batch gradient is the sample mean of individual sample gradients. By the CLT, the mini-batch gradient is approximately normally distributed around the true gradient. Larger batch sizes give more accurate (lower variance) gradient estimates. Smaller batch sizes give noisier but faster updates — the noise can help escape local minima.

Limitations: the CLT requires (1) i.i.d. samples — not satisfied for correlated time series data, (2) finite variance — not satisfied for heavy-tailed distributions (Cauchy distribution has infinite variance; the CLT does not apply), (3) large n — for very small samples (n < 20) with non-normal data, the approximation may be poor. For small samples, use non-parametric tests.

Worked example

    sample_means = [np.mean(np.random.exponential(scale=2, size=n)) for _ in range(5000)]

CV confidence interval:

Why this matters in AI
The CLT is the mathematical justification for using normal-distribution-based statistical tests on non-normal data, constructing confidence intervals, and understanding why larger samples give better estimates. It is foundational to evaluating ML models statistically.
Common mistakes
  • Applying CLT to dependent samples — CLT requires independence. Time series data where each observation depends on the previous violates this. Use time-series-specific tests instead.
  • Using CLT for very small samples — for n < 20 and non-normal data, the normal approximation is poor. Use the t-distribution or bootstrap confidence intervals.
  • Forgetting that CLT is about sample MEANS, not individual samples — the CLT does not say individual observations become normally distributed. It says sample MEANS do.

Hypothesis testing

Detailed explanation

Hypothesis testing is a formal statistical framework for making decisions about whether an observed effect is real or could plausibly have occurred by chance. The null hypothesis (H₀) states 'there is no effect' or 'no difference exists'. The alternative hypothesis (H₁) states 'there is an effect'. The test computes a p-value: the probability of observing results at least as extreme as the data if H₀ were true.

If the p-value is below a significance threshold (typically α=0.05), we reject H₀. This does NOT mean the alternative is proven true — it means the data is unlikely under H₀. The significance level α is the pre-specified rate of false positives (Type I errors). β is the rate of false negatives (Type II errors). Statistical power = 1 - β is the probability of correctly detecting a true effect.

Common tests: t-test (compare means of two groups — is the average effect of drug A significantly different from drug B?), chi-square test (are two categorical variables independent?), ANOVA (compare means across 3+ groups), Wilcoxon signed-rank test (non-parametric alternative to t-test when normality is violated).

In ML, hypothesis testing is used in: A/B testing (is new model B significantly better than baseline A?), feature selection (is this feature's correlation with the target statistically significant?), and model comparison (are two models significantly different in performance across cross-validation folds?). Use paired t-test to compare two models' CV scores — each fold is a pair.

Statistical significance vs practical significance: with a large enough dataset, even a tiny meaningless difference can be statistically significant (p < 0.05). A difference of 0.001 in model accuracy may be statistically significant on 1 million examples but practically irrelevant for a business decision. Always report effect size alongside p-values.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

t-test: from scipy.stats import ttest_ind
group_a = [72, 68, 75, 70, 65]  # model A accuracy scores per CV fold
group_b = [78, 80, 74, 82, 79]  # model B accuracy scores
t_stat, p_value = ttest_ind(group_a, group_b)
print(f't={t_stat:.3f}, p={p_value:.4f}')
If p < 0.05: reject H₀ (model B is significantly better). If p >= 0.05: fail to reject H₀.
Paired t-test for model comparison: from scipy.stats import ttest_rel
t_stat, p_val = ttest_rel(cv_scores_modelA, cv_scores_modelB)
A/B test: chi2 test for conversion rate. from scipy.stats import chi2_contingency
contingency = [[n_A_convert, n_A_no_convert], [n_B_convert, n_B_no_convert]]
chi2, p, dof, expected = chi2_contingency(contingency)
Why this matters in AI
Hypothesis testing distinguishes genuine improvements from random noise. Without it, you might deploy a new model because it 'looked better' on a test set — when the difference was not statistically meaningful. A/B testing and model comparison both require hypothesis testing to make sound decisions.
Common mistakes
  • Using p < 0.05 as proof of a meaningful effect — p-value only tells you about the probability under H₀, not the size or importance of the effect. Always compute Cohen's d or percent improvement.
  • Running many tests and only reporting significant ones (p-hacking) — if you run 20 tests at α=0.05, you expect 1 false positive. Use Bonferroni correction or FDR control for multiple comparisons.
  • Using a t-test on non-normal data with small samples — for n < 30 with non-normal data, use non-parametric tests (Wilcoxon, Mann-Whitney).

p-value

Detailed explanation

A p-value is the probability of observing test results at least as extreme as the observed results, assuming the null hypothesis (H₀) is true. If p=0.03, it means: if H₀ were true, there is only a 3% chance of observing an effect this large or larger just by random chance. We typically reject H₀ when p < 0.05 (a conventional threshold). A small p-value is evidence against H₀ — it says the observed effect is unlikely under H₀.

What p-values are NOT: the probability that H₀ is true, the probability that the result is 'real', or the size of the effect. A p-value of 0.001 does not mean the effect is large — with enough data, even a tiny difference produces p < 0.001. Statistical significance (small p-value) and practical significance (large effect size) are different and both matter.

In ML: p-values appear in (1) t-tests comparing two model's CV scores: is model B significantly better than model A? (2) feature selection: is this feature's correlation with the target statistically significant? (3) A/B testing: is the conversion rate difference between two models statistically significant?

Multiple comparisons problem: if you test 100 hypotheses simultaneously at α=0.05, you expect 5 false positives even if all null hypotheses are true. This is p-hacking. Solutions: Bonferroni correction (divide α by the number of tests — very conservative), Benjamini-Hochberg FDR control (controls the proportion of false discoveries — less conservative, preferred for feature selection and hypothesis generation).

The replication crisis: many published results with p < 0.05 failed to replicate. Reasons: underpowered studies (too few samples to reliably detect the effect), p-hacking (testing many things and only reporting significant results), and publication bias (only significant results are published). For ML: report effect sizes and confidence intervals alongside p-values, and use pre-registered analysis plans for important decisions.

Why this matters in AI
P-values are the universal language of statistical inference. They appear in model comparisons, A/B tests, feature selection, and academic papers. Understanding what p-values mean (and don't mean) is essential to avoid drawing wrong conclusions from statistical tests.
Common mistakes
  • Treating p < 0.05 as binary proof — p = 0.049 and p = 0.051 should lead to similar conclusions. The 0.05 threshold is a convention, not a law of nature.
  • Not correcting for multiple comparisons — testing 1000 features at α=0.05 gives 50 false positive significant features. Use FDR correction for any multiple testing scenario.
  • Confusing p-value with effect size — a p-value of 1e-10 means the result is extremely unlikely under H₀, not that the effect is large. Always report effect size (Cohen's d, Pearson r, relative lift) alongside p-values.

Confidence interval

Detailed explanation

A confidence interval (CI) provides a range of plausible values for an unknown population parameter, based on observed sample data. A 95% confidence interval means: if we repeated this sampling procedure 100 times and computed a CI each time, approximately 95 of the 100 intervals would contain the true parameter. It is NOT the probability that the true value lies in this particular interval (the true value either is or is not in the interval — no probability).

Computing a CI for the mean: CI = x̄ ± t* * (s/sqrt(n)) where x̄ is the sample mean, s is the sample std, n is sample size, and t* is the critical value from the t-distribution (1.96 for 95% CI with large n). For CV accuracy: if mean=0.82, std=0.05, n=10, then CI = 0.82 ± 1.96 * (0.05/sqrt(10)) = 0.82 ± 0.031 = [0.789, 0.851]. Report this interval, not just the mean.

Bootstrap confidence intervals are more robust and require no distributional assumptions. Bootstrap: sample n examples with replacement from the data 10,000 times. For each bootstrap sample, compute the statistic. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the bootstrap distribution form the 95% CI. Bootstrap CIs are appropriate for any statistic (not just means) and any sample size.

In ML, CI matters for: reporting model performance (always report CI not just point estimate), A/B testing (the CI for the performance difference must not include 0 to be significant), and hyperparameter tuning (if two hyperparameter configurations have overlapping CIs, the difference may not be meaningful).

Margin of error is half the CI width: the CI [0.789, 0.851] has margin of error 0.031. A smaller margin of error means more precision in the estimate. To halve the margin of error, you need 4× more data (because precision scales as 1/sqrt(n)). This relationship guides decisions about how much data to collect.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

from scipy import stats
scores = np.array([0.85, 0.82, 0.88, 0.79, 0.86, 0.83, 0.81, 0.87, 0.84, 0.80])
ci = stats.t.interval(0.95, df=len(scores)-1, loc=np.mean(scores), scale=stats.sem(scores))
print(f'Mean: {np.mean(scores):.4f}, 95% CI: [{ci[0]:.4f}, {ci[1]:.4f}]')
Bootstrap CI: def bootstrap_ci(data, n_bootstrap=10000, alpha=0.95):
    boot_means = [np.mean(np.random.choice(data, size=len(data), replace=True)) for _ in range(n_bootstrap)]
    lo, hi = np.percentile(boot_means, [(1-alpha)/2*100, (1+alpha)/2*100])
    return lo, hi
Why this matters in AI
A point estimate (single number) without a confidence interval is an incomplete result. A model with mean accuracy 0.82 could have CI [0.70, 0.94] (very uncertain) or [0.80, 0.84] (very precise). The CI determines whether a performance difference between two models is statistically meaningful.
Common mistakes
  • Interpreting CI as 'there is a 95% probability the true value is in this interval' — the frequentist CI is about the procedure, not about the probability for a specific interval.
  • Using a 95% CI because it is the standard without considering the context — for safety-critical applications (medical AI), use 99% or 99.9% CIs.
  • Comparing point estimates without CI — model A = 0.82, model B = 0.84 does NOT mean B is better. Check if their CIs overlap.

A/B testing

Detailed explanation

A/B testing (also called randomized controlled experiment) is the method of comparing two versions of something (A = control, B = treatment) by randomly assigning users to one version and measuring outcomes. In ML, A/B tests compare two models, two recommendation algorithms, two UI designs, or two pricing strategies. Randomization ensures that differences in outcomes are caused by the version, not by confounding factors.

A correctly designed A/B test requires: (1) a clear metric (conversion rate, session duration, revenue per user), (2) a pre-specified significance level α (typically 0.05) and power (1-β, typically 0.8), (3) a power analysis to determine the required sample size before collecting data, (4) random assignment to groups, and (5) analysis after the pre-specified sample size is reached (not before). Stopping early or peeking at results leads to false positives.

Sample size calculation: the required number of samples depends on the baseline rate, the minimum detectable effect (MDE — the smallest improvement worth detecting), desired power, and significance level. For a conversion rate test (baseline=5%, MDE=1%, α=0.05, power=0.8), you need ~5000 samples per group. Underpowering leads to inconclusive results; overpowering wastes resources.

Pitfalls: novelty effect (new versions get higher engagement temporarily because users are curious, not because they are genuinely better — wait until behavior stabilizes), Simpsons paradox (the winning version in total may lose in every subgroup — check segmented results), and network effects (in social platforms, user A assigned to B may interact with user A2 assigned to A, contaminating the groups).

Multi-armed bandit (MAB) testing is an alternative to A/B testing that dynamically allocates more traffic to better-performing variants during the experiment, reducing regret (lost conversions from showing inferior variants). MAB is appropriate when the cost of showing the worse version is high (medical treatment, high-value recommendations). Pure A/B testing is appropriate when you need clean statistical inference.

Analyze: from scipy.stats import ttest_ind or chi2_contingency
Report: p-value, effect size (Cohen's d or relative lift), 95% confidence interval.
Decision: if p < 0.05 AND practical significance: deploy B. if p >= 0.05: keep A or run longer. if significant but tiny effect: cost-benefit analysis.
Why this matters in AI
A/B testing is the gold standard for making data-driven product decisions. Without it, product changes are made based on intuition. With it, you can distinguish genuine improvements from noise, estimate the size of the effect, and make confident deployment decisions.
Common mistakes
  • Peeking at results before the predetermined sample size is reached and stopping early — this inflates false positive rates dramatically. The p-value is only valid at the predetermined sample size.
  • Not checking group balance — if randomization is imperfect (assignment bug), the groups may differ on baseline characteristics, biasing results. Always check that groups have similar baseline metrics.
  • Running too many simultaneous tests — testing 20 features simultaneously at α=0.05 produces ~1 false positive. Use family-wise error rate control or FDR correction.

Correlation vs causation

Detailed explanation

Correlation measures the statistical association between two variables: when one changes, does the other tend to change? Causation means one variable directly causes changes in the other. 'Correlation does not imply causation' is one of the most important principles in data science — observed associations may be entirely due to confounding variables, reverse causation, or coincidence.

Types of correlation: Pearson correlation measures linear relationship (-1 to 1). Spearman correlation measures monotonic (rank) relationship — more robust to outliers. Kendall's tau measures ordinal concordance. Each captures different aspects of dependence. A Pearson r=0 means no LINEAR relationship but could miss a strong nonlinear one (e.g., parabola).

Confounders: ice cream sales and drowning rates are correlated — not because ice cream causes drowning, but because both are caused by summer heat (the confounder). In ML: shoe size and reading ability are correlated in a dataset of humans of all ages — the confounder is age. Including the confounder in the model removes the spurious correlation.

Reverse causation: hospital patients are sicker than people who don't go to hospitals. If you observe 'going to hospital → worse health outcomes,' the causal arrow is reversed — sick people go to hospitals, hospitals don't make people sick. In ML: if user engagement is correlated with having more friends, does more friends cause engagement, or does engagement attract friends?

Causal inference methods: Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs) are the gold standard — randomly assign treatment to break confounding. Observational study methods: difference-in-differences (compare change before/after treatment between treated and control groups), instrumental variables, propensity score matching. In A/B testing for products, you're essentially running an RCT: random assignment breaks the correlation between user characteristics and treatment assignment.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from scipy import stats
np.random.seed(42)
n = 1000
# Spurious correlation via confounder
confound = np.random.normal(0, 1, n)  # e.g., 'age'
X = confound + np.random.normal(0, 0.3, n)  # ice cream sales
Y = confound + np.random.normal(0, 0.3, n)  # drowning rate
print(f'Correlation X,Y: {np.corrcoef(X, Y)[0,1]:.3f}')  # strong positive: ~0.85
# After controlling for confounder (partial correlation)
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression
X_resid = X - LinearRegression().fit(confound.reshape(-1,1), X).predict(confound.reshape(-1,1))
Y_resid = Y - LinearRegression().fit(confound.reshape(-1,1), Y).predict(confound.reshape(-1,1))
print(f'Partial correlation (controlling for confound): {np.corrcoef(X_resid, Y_resid)[0,1]:.3f}')  # near 0
Why this matters in AI
Causal reasoning is what separates data scientists who make good decisions from those who make mistakes. A model that learns a spurious correlation will generalize poorly when the correlation breaks down. Building a recommendation system based on correlation (users who like A also like B) is fine for personalization; building a policy decision based on that same correlation is dangerous if B doesn't actually cause satisfaction.
Common mistakes
  • Using correlation to select features for causal models — feature A correlates with the target but only because of confounder C. If C is not in the training data, A will appear predictive. In production, when C changes, A's correlation with the target will break.
  • Running many correlations and reporting the significant ones — with 100 features, 5 will correlate with the target at p<0.05 by chance. Always correct for multiple comparisons or validate correlations on a held-out set.
  • Confusing predictive models with causal models — a model that predicts churn well does not tell you what to do to prevent churn. Intervening on a correlated feature (send discount to users predicted to churn) might not actually prevent churn if the feature is not causally related.
