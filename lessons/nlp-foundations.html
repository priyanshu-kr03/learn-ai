Tokenization

Detailed explanation

Tokenization is the process of splitting raw text into smaller units called tokens before any model can process it. A token can be a word, a subword fragment, a character, or a punctuation mark depending on the tokenizer design. Every NLP pipeline begins here because models cannot work on raw strings; they work on sequences of numbered tokens.

Word tokenization splits on spaces and punctuation, giving one token per word. This breaks down for unknown words like brand names or typos. Subword tokenization (used in BERT, GPT, and most modern LLMs) splits rare words into smaller known pieces. For example, 'unbelievable' might become ['un', '##believ', '##able'] in BERT's tokenizer. This way the vocabulary stays manageable even for multilingual text.

Character tokenization treats each letter as a token. It handles any word, but sequences become very long and the model must learn word meaning from scratch. Byte-pair encoding (BPE) and WordPiece strike the middle ground: they start from characters and merge frequent pairs until the vocabulary reaches a target size.

Token count directly affects cost and context limits in LLM APIs. The sentence 'The quick brown fox' is roughly 4–5 tokens in GPT tokenizers. A 10-page PDF might be 4,000–6,000 tokens. Knowing this helps you plan chunking, estimate cost, and avoid hitting context limits.

Why this matters in AI
Every downstream NLP task — classification, translation, summarization, RAG — depends on tokenization. Bad tokenization loses meaning before the model even starts.
Common mistakes
  • Assuming one word equals one token — subword tokenizers often split words, so token count is always higher than word count.
  • Tokenizing the full dataset before the train/test split — the tokenizer's vocabulary should be fixed before seeing test data.
  • Ignoring special tokens like [CLS], [SEP], or <|endoftext|> that models require but add to token count.

Stop words

Detailed explanation

Stop words are common words like 'the', 'is', 'at', 'which' that appear in almost every document and carry little discriminating information. Classic NLP pipelines remove them to reduce vocabulary size and noise before building features like Bag of Words or TF-IDF.

The decision to remove stop words depends on the task. For topic classification and keyword extraction, removing 'the' and 'a' is usually safe. For sentiment analysis, removing 'not' would flip meaning entirely — 'not good' becomes 'good'. For question answering and modern transformer models, stop words are usually kept because the model learns context from them.

Most NLP libraries include a default stop word list. NLTK has 179 English stop words. SpaCy's list is larger. You can always customize: add domain-specific filler words ('please', 'kindly' in support tickets) or remove words from the default list that matter for your task.

Modern LLMs and transformer-based classifiers typically do not need stop word removal because tokenization and attention mechanisms handle frequency naturally. Stop word removal is most useful in TF-IDF pipelines, search engines, and keyword extraction.

Why this matters in AI
Stop word choices silently affect model quality. Keeping or removing the wrong words changes classification accuracy, especially for sentiment, intent, and negation tasks.
Common mistakes
  • Using a default stop word list without reviewing it for your domain.
  • Removing 'not', 'no', 'never' — these flip meaning and should usually be kept for sentiment tasks.
  • Applying stop word removal to transformer models that do not need it, wasting preprocessing time.

Stemming

Detailed explanation

Stemming reduces words to a root form by stripping suffixes using simple rules, not a dictionary. 'Running', 'runs', and 'runner' all stem to 'run'. 'Happiness' becomes 'happi'. The result is not always a real word, but it groups related forms together for counting and matching.

The Porter stemmer (1980) is the most widely used English stemmer. It applies a sequence of rules like: strip 'ing', strip 'ed', strip 'ness'. It is fast and deterministic but makes errors. 'Argue' becomes 'argu'. 'Studies' becomes 'studi'. These broken stems still work for bag-of-words features because both 'studying' and 'studies' become 'studi' and are counted together.

Stemming is faster than lemmatization because it needs no dictionary lookup. For large-scale keyword extraction, search indexing, or TF-IDF features on millions of documents, stemming is a practical choice when slight inaccuracy is acceptable.

Modern deep learning pipelines rarely use stemming because subword tokenizers and embedding models handle morphological variation through training. Stemming matters most in classic IR pipelines, search engines, and TF-IDF based classifiers.

Why this matters in AI
Stemming normalizes text so word count features treat 'run', 'running', and 'runs' as the same concept, improving keyword matching and TF-IDF quality.
Common mistakes
  • Using stemming when you need real words — stems like 'happi' are not valid English and break readability.
  • Applying stemming to transformer-based models that tokenize subwords — it adds no benefit and can corrupt inputs.
  • Conflating stemming with lemmatization — stemming is rule-based and crude; lemmatization is dictionary-based and linguistically correct.

Lemmatization

Detailed explanation

Lemmatization reduces a word to its dictionary base form (lemma) using vocabulary and part-of-speech information. 'Running' becomes 'run'. 'Better' becomes 'good'. 'Was' becomes 'be'. Unlike stemming, the result is always a valid word.

To lemmatize correctly, the system needs to know the part of speech. 'Meeting' as a verb lemmatizes to 'meet'. 'Meeting' as a noun stays 'meeting'. Without POS tagging, lemmatizers default to a single reading and may be wrong. SpaCy handles this automatically by running POS tagging before lemmatization.

Lemmatization is slower than stemming because it queries a morphological database (like WordNet). For most production NLP tasks with modern transformers, neither stemming nor lemmatization is needed — the model handles word forms through pre-training. These techniques matter in TF-IDF pipelines, search, and classical NLP.

SpaCy is the most popular tool for lemmatization in Python. It runs a full NLP pipeline (tokenize, POS tag, lemmatize) in one call. NLTK's WordNetLemmatizer works but requires manual POS input for best results.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

Input: 'The geese were flying better than expected.'
SpaCy lemmatization: ['the', 'goose', 'be', 'fly', 'well', 'than', 'expect'].

Why this matters in AI
Lemmatization ensures 'go', 'goes', 'went', 'going' all map to 'go', so frequency counts and embeddings treat them as the same concept.
Common mistakes
  • Using the NLTK WordNetLemmatizer without providing POS tags — it defaults to noun and gives wrong results for verbs.
  • Lemmatizing text that will be fed into a BERT or GPT model — unnecessary preprocessing that may degrade performance.
  • Confusing lemmatization with stemming — lemmatization always produces real words; stemming does not.

n-grams

Detailed explanation

An n-gram is a contiguous sequence of n tokens from a text. Unigrams are single words. Bigrams are pairs of adjacent words. Trigrams are groups of three. N-grams capture local context that single words miss: 'not good' as a bigram carries a different meaning than 'not' and 'good' counted separately.

N-gram features are used in bag-of-words classifiers, language models, and spam filters. Adding bigrams to a TF-IDF spam classifier often improves accuracy because phrases like 'click here', 'free money', and 'limited offer' are strong spam signals that individual words cannot capture.

Character n-grams work on character sequences instead of words. They are useful for language identification, typo detection, and handling unknown words. The character trigrams in 'hello' are: 'hel', 'ell', 'llo'. Character n-grams are robust to spelling variations.

The trade-off with n-grams is sparsity. A corpus with 10,000 unique words has 10,000 unigrams but potentially 100 million possible bigrams, most of which never appear. This is why n-gram models need smoothing or feature selection, and why they have been largely replaced by embeddings for dense representation tasks.

Why this matters in AI
N-grams let bag-of-words models capture short phrases and negations without needing a full neural network.
Common mistakes
  • Using only unigrams for sentiment analysis — negations like 'not good' are invisible to unigram models.
  • Using very large n (4+) without regularization — the feature space explodes and most features appear only once.
  • Applying n-grams to transformer models — they learn context internally and n-gram features add no value.

Bag of Words

Detailed explanation

Bag of Words (BoW) represents a document as a vector of word counts, ignoring grammar and word order. Each dimension of the vector corresponds to one word in the vocabulary. If the vocabulary has 10,000 words, every document becomes a 10,000-dimensional vector where most values are zero.

To build a BoW model: collect all unique words across documents to form the vocabulary, then for each document count how many times each vocabulary word appears. The result is a sparse matrix where rows are documents and columns are vocabulary words.

BoW is simple and interpretable. A spam classifier trained on BoW features can explain its decision by showing which words most contributed to the spam score. It works well for short documents with distinctive vocabulary. It fails when word order matters (sarcasm, negation) and when vocabulary is very large.

BoW is the foundation for TF-IDF, which improves it by weighting rare words more strongly than common ones. Before embeddings dominated NLP, BoW with TF-IDF was the standard baseline for text classification. It is still a useful fast baseline today.

Documents: ['I love Python', 'Python is great', 'I love great food']
Vocabulary: ['I', 'Python', 'food', 'great', 'is', 'love']

Doc 2 vector: [0, 1, 0, 1, 1, 0] — 'Python'=1, 'great'=1, 'is'=1
Doc 3 vector: [1, 0, 1, 1, 0, 1] — 'I'=1, 'food'=1, 'great'=1, 'love'=1
Why this matters in AI
BoW turns text into numbers that ML models can process. It is the simplest text representation and a required baseline before trying embeddings.
Common mistakes
  • Using BoW without removing very frequent words (stop words) — 'the' and 'a' dominate every vector and add noise.
  • Building the vocabulary on the full dataset including test data — causes data leakage.
  • Comparing BoW vectors by dot product without normalization — longer documents always score higher regardless of content.

TF-IDF

Detailed explanation

TF-IDF stands for Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency. It improves on raw word counts by giving higher weight to words that are frequent in one document but rare across the whole corpus. A word like 'refund' that appears often in one complaint email but rarely elsewhere gets a high TF-IDF score — it is informative. A word like 'the' appears everywhere and gets a very low score.

Term Frequency (TF) is simply how often a word appears in a document, often normalized by document length. Inverse Document Frequency (IDF) is the log of total documents divided by the number of documents containing the word. Multiplying TF × IDF gives each word a score that balances local frequency with global rarity.

TF-IDF is used in search engines, keyword extraction, document similarity, and text classification. In a search engine, TF-IDF ranks documents by how well their words match the query's rare terms. In keyword extraction, the highest TF-IDF words in a document are its most distinctive terms.

Sklearn's TfidfVectorizer applies tokenization, stop word removal, TF-IDF weighting, and L2 normalization in one step. The resulting matrix is sparse and suitable for logistic regression, SVM, or naive Bayes classifiers. For modern transformers, TF-IDF is replaced by learned embeddings, but it remains an excellent and fast baseline.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

3 documents: ['the cat sat', 'the dog ran fast', 'the cat ran fast']
IDF of 'the': log(3/3) = 0. It appears in all docs — worthless.
IDF of 'cat': log(3/2) ≈ 0.41. Appears in 2 of 3 docs.
IDF of 'fast': log(3/2) ≈ 0.41. Appears in 2 of 3 docs.
TF-IDF of 'cat' in Doc 1: TF=1/3, IDF=0.41 → score ≈ 0.14. This word matters for Doc 1 specifically.
Why this matters in AI
TF-IDF filters noise (common words score near zero) and highlights signal (rare but frequent words score high), making text features much more informative than raw counts.
Common mistakes
  • Using TF-IDF without fitting on training data only — fitting on test data leaks information about the test corpus.
  • Ignoring the max_features parameter — a 500,000-word vocabulary creates huge sparse matrices that slow training.
  • Treating TF-IDF scores as probabilities — they are relative weights, not probabilities.
Practice task

Word embeddings

Detailed explanation

Word embeddings represent words as dense vectors of real numbers in a high-dimensional space (typically 50–300 dimensions). Unlike BoW, where each word is an independent feature, embeddings place similar words close together in vector space. 'King' and 'queen' are nearby. 'Dog' and 'cat' are nearby. 'Bank' (river) and 'bank' (finance) end up in different regions depending on context.

Embeddings are learned from large text corpora by training a model to predict words from their context (Word2Vec, FastText) or to co-occur with similar contexts (GloVe). The resulting vectors capture syntax and semantics. The famous example: king − man + woman ≈ queen. The direction 'gender' appears to be encoded in the embedding space.

Static embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe, FastText) assign one vector per word regardless of context. 'Bank' always has the same vector whether in 'river bank' or 'bank account'. Contextual embeddings (BERT, GPT) produce different vectors for the same word in different sentences, capturing polysemy.

Pre-trained embeddings let you start with meaningful representations without training from scratch. Loading 300-dimensional GloVe vectors trained on 840 billion tokens gives your model a rich starting point even with a small training dataset.

results = vector_db.search(query_vector, top_k=5)
Why this matters in AI
Embeddings let models understand semantic similarity without seeing every word pair in training. They are the foundation of modern NLP.
Common mistakes
  • Using embeddings trained on a different domain without fine-tuning — medical embeddings and Twitter embeddings represent very different text.
  • Averaging word embeddings for sentences without weighting by TF-IDF — common words dominate the average.
  • Expecting static embeddings to handle polysemy — 'bank' has one vector for both meanings.

Word2Vec GloVe FastText

Detailed explanation

Word2Vec trains a shallow neural network to predict a target word from its context words (CBOW) or predict context words from a target word (Skip-gram). The internal weight matrix, after training, becomes the word embeddings. Trained by Google on Google News (100 billion words), the 300-dimensional vectors capture rich semantic relationships.

GloVe (Global Vectors) trains on word co-occurrence counts across the entire corpus. It factorizes a co-occurrence matrix so that the dot product of two word vectors approximates the log probability of one word appearing near the other. GloVe is faster to train than Word2Vec on the same corpus and often performs comparably on analogy tasks.

FastText, developed by Facebook, extends Word2Vec by representing each word as a sum of its character n-gram vectors. This means FastText can produce embeddings for words it never saw in training by decomposing them into n-grams. 'unseen_word' can be embedded using its character pieces. This makes FastText excellent for morphologically rich languages, typo-heavy text, and low-resource settings.

All three produce static embeddings. They have been largely superseded by contextual embeddings (BERT, RoBERTa, GPT) for most NLP tasks, but they remain useful for fast baselines, large-vocabulary tasks, and settings where full transformer fine-tuning is too expensive.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

Word2Vec: Train on sentences with window=5. Every word gets a 300-dim vector.
GloVe: Build co-occurrence matrix, then factorize with matrix decomposition.
FastText: For the word 'playing', character 3-grams are ['pla','lay','ayi','yin','ing']. Sum their vectors.
Unknown word in FastText: 'playng' (typo) → use its character n-grams to estimate a vector. Word2Vec would return nothing.
Why this matters in AI
Choosing the right embedding method affects downstream model quality. FastText handles typos; GloVe is fast to train; Word2Vec is the most widely documented baseline.
Common mistakes
  • Using Word2Vec for a language with rich morphology (Turkish, Finnish) — FastText handles word forms much better.
  • Not normalizing embeddings before computing cosine similarity — raw dot products reward longer vectors.
  • Expecting these static embeddings to capture context — 'I went to the bank' and 'river bank' both produce the same vector for 'bank'.

Text classification

Detailed explanation

Text classification assigns a label to a piece of text from a fixed set of categories. Spam detection (spam/not spam), sentiment analysis (positive/negative/neutral), topic categorization (sports/politics/tech), intent detection (book_flight/cancel_order) — all are text classification tasks.

The pipeline is: preprocess text → extract features (TF-IDF or embeddings) → train a classifier (logistic regression, SVM, or fine-tuned transformer) → evaluate on a held-out test set. For small datasets and simple categories, TF-IDF + logistic regression is a strong baseline. For complex tasks or limited labels, a pre-trained transformer like BERT fine-tuned on your categories usually wins.

Evaluation metrics matter here. Accuracy is misleading when classes are imbalanced — a model that always predicts 'not spam' on a 95% ham dataset gets 95% accuracy while being useless. Use precision, recall, and F1 per class. For multi-class problems, use macro or weighted F1.

Zero-shot classification with models like RoBERTa-MNLI lets you classify without any labeled training examples by framing the task as natural language inference. This is useful when you cannot collect labeled data quickly.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

Feature extraction: TfidfVectorizer(max_features=5000, ngram_range=(1,2)).

Error analysis: inspect the 20 most confidently wrong predictions to find systematic failure patterns.
Why this matters in AI
Text classification is one of the most common NLP tasks in production — routing tickets, filtering content, detecting intent. Getting the evaluation right prevents shipping a model that looks good but fails on the most important cases.
Common mistakes
  • Reporting only accuracy on imbalanced datasets — the majority-class baseline often beats a real model on accuracy alone.
  • Not doing error analysis — the model's confident wrong predictions reveal whether the labels are clean or the features are insufficient.
  • Fine-tuning BERT before trying TF-IDF + logistic regression — the simpler baseline often closes 80% of the gap at 1% of the compute cost.
Practice task

Sentiment analysis

Detailed explanation

Sentiment analysis predicts the emotional tone of text — typically positive, negative, or neutral, but sometimes a 5-star rating, an emotion (anger/joy/sadness), or a continuous sentiment score. It is one of the most commonly deployed NLP tasks: product reviews, social media monitoring, customer feedback, and brand perception all rely on it.

Simple approaches train a binary classifier (positive/negative) on labeled reviews. The model learns words like 'excellent', 'love', 'broken', 'disappointed' as strong signals. Adding bigrams captures patterns like 'not great' and 'could be better'. The challenge is that sentiment is context-dependent: 'This movie is not as bad as everyone says' is positive despite containing 'bad'.

Aspect-based sentiment analysis goes deeper: instead of one label per review, it identifies sentiment toward specific aspects. A hotel review might be positive about the location but negative about the WiFi. This requires extracting both the aspect and its sentiment, which is a more complex structured prediction problem.

Modern approaches fine-tune BERT or RoBERTa on labeled sentiment data. These models handle negation, sarcasm, and domain-specific language far better than TF-IDF classifiers. For quick deployment without labeled data, Hugging Face's sentiment-analysis pipeline uses a pre-trained model with no fine-tuning needed.

Naive BoW prediction: many positive words ('fast') may push toward positive.

Why this matters in AI
Sentiment is a core business signal. A product with many 4-star reviews that mention 'battery life' as negative is actionable insight. Raw star ratings hide the why.
Common mistakes
  • Ignoring negations — 'not good', 'not at all satisfied' flip sentiment but naive models often miss this.
  • Training on movie reviews and deploying on product reviews — domain shift degrades accuracy significantly.
  • Treating neutral as the absence of sentiment — neutral texts actively signal no strong opinion and need their own class.

NER

Detailed explanation

Named Entity Recognition (NER) identifies and classifies named entities in text into predefined categories: PERSON, ORGANIZATION, LOCATION, DATE, MONEY, PRODUCT, etc. In the sentence 'Apple paid Tim Cook $100M in 2023', NER should extract: Apple (ORG), Tim Cook (PERSON), $100M (MONEY), 2023 (DATE).

NER is a sequence labeling problem. Each token gets a label, typically using the BIO scheme: B (beginning of entity), I (inside entity), O (outside any entity). 'Tim' gets B-PERSON, 'Cook' gets I-PERSON, 'Apple' gets B-ORG, 'paid' gets O.

SpaCy ships with pre-trained NER models for English and many other languages. You can use them directly or fine-tune on domain-specific labels. Medical NER might add DISEASE, DRUG, GENE. Legal NER might add STATUTE, COURT, JUDGE. Off-the-shelf models trained on news will struggle with specialized domains.

NER is used for information extraction from contracts and reports, knowledge graph construction, entity linking (connecting a mention to a Wikipedia entry), and question answering (find all dates mentioned). Evaluation uses entity-level F1, not token-level accuracy, because a partial match on a multi-word entity is still wrong.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

Input: 'Elon Musk founded SpaceX in 2002 in Hawthorne, California.'
BIO labels: 'Elon'=B-PERSON, 'Musk'=I-PERSON, 'founded'=O, 'SpaceX'=B-ORG, 'in'=O, '2002'=B-DATE, 'in'=O, 'Hawthorne'=B-GPE, ','=O, 'California'=B-GPE.
SpaCy: import spacy; nlp = spacy.load('en_core_web_sm'); [(ent.text, ent.label_) for ent in nlp(text).ents]
Check precision and recall at entity level — partial matches count as false positives AND false negatives.
Why this matters in AI
NER turns unstructured text into structured data. Extracting company names, dates, and amounts from thousands of contracts is only feasible with NER.
Common mistakes
  • Evaluating NER with token-level accuracy — a model that gets 'Tim' right but misses 'Cook' still gets a partial token accuracy credit.
  • Using a news-trained model on medical or legal text without fine-tuning — entity types and boundary rules are domain-specific.
  • Ignoring nested entities — 'New York Stock Exchange' is both a LOCATION and an ORGANIZATION depending on context.

Question answering

Detailed explanation

Question answering (QA) takes a question and a context passage and returns the answer. Extractive QA finds the answer span directly in the passage — the model predicts start and end token positions. Abstractive QA generates the answer as new text. RAG-based QA retrieves relevant passages first and then generates an answer grounded in them.

The SQuAD benchmark (Stanford Question Answering Dataset) made extractive QA popular. A BERT model fine-tuned on SQuAD can find answers in a passage with high accuracy. But SQuAD-style QA assumes the answer is always in the passage. In the real world, the answer might not exist — the model needs to say 'I don't know'.

Open-domain QA combines retrieval with reading comprehension. A retriever finds relevant documents from a large corpus. A reader extracts or generates the answer from those documents. This is essentially RAG applied to question answering. The quality of both retrieval and reading matter — weak retrieval means the reader never sees the relevant passage.

Evaluation uses Exact Match (EM) — answer exactly matches the ground truth — and F1 score computed over token overlap. F1 handles paraphrases better than EM. For generative QA, use ROUGE or human evaluation.

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

Passage: 'The Eiffel Tower was built in 1889 for the World's Fair in Paris.'

Evaluate: EM = 1 if prediction == '1889', else 0. F1 measures token overlap.
Why this matters in AI
QA is the backbone of enterprise search, document assistants, and support chatbots. Understanding the difference between extractive and generative QA determines which architecture you build.
Common mistakes
  • Assuming the answer is always in the context — real QA systems must handle unanswerable questions.
  • Using EM as the only metric — paraphrased correct answers fail EM but should score well.
  • Ignoring retrieval quality in open-domain QA — the best reader cannot recover from bad retrieval.

Summarization

Detailed explanation

Summarization compresses a long document into a shorter version that retains the key information. Extractive summarization selects and concatenates important sentences from the original text. Abstractive summarization generates new sentences that may not appear in the original — like how a human would summarize.

Extractive methods score sentences by importance (using TF-IDF, graph-based algorithms like TextRank, or sentence embeddings) and select the top-k. They are fast and never hallucinate because output text is directly copied. Abstractive methods use seq2seq models (T5, BART, Pegasus) trained on document-summary pairs. They can rephrase and synthesize but may introduce inaccuracies.

Evaluation uses ROUGE scores. ROUGE-1 measures unigram overlap between generated and reference summary. ROUGE-2 measures bigram overlap. ROUGE-L measures longest common subsequence. High ROUGE does not always mean a good summary — it rewards copying reference words, not conceptual accuracy.

For production summarization of long documents (legal contracts, research papers), chunking is required because transformer models have context limits. Chunk the document, summarize each chunk, then summarize the summaries — map-reduce summarization. LangChain implements this pattern.

3-paragraph article → goal: 2-sentence summary.

Check: does the abstractive summary add any facts not in the original? If yes, that is hallucination.
Why this matters in AI
Summarization reduces reading time and is a gateway to RAG — breaking documents into summaries before indexing improves retrieval quality.
Common mistakes
  • Evaluating only with ROUGE without reading the outputs — high ROUGE summaries can still be incoherent or miss the main point.
  • Using abstractive models on factually critical content (medical, legal) without fact-checking — hallucination is a real risk.
  • Not handling document length — most seq2seq models truncate inputs beyond 512 or 1024 tokens.

Translation

Detailed explanation

Machine translation converts text from one language to another. Early statistical methods aligned word pairs across parallel corpora. Neural machine translation (NMT) with encoder-decoder architectures dramatically improved fluency and accuracy. Today's strongest translators are large transformer models (Google Translate, DeepL, NLLB) trained on hundreds of billions of sentence pairs.

The transformer architecture was originally proposed for translation in the 2017 paper 'Attention Is All You Need'. An encoder reads the source sentence into contextual representations. A decoder generates the target sentence one token at a time, attending to the encoder output at each step. This attention mechanism allows the model to align 'bank' in the source with the correct word in the target based on context.

Evaluation uses BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy). BLEU measures n-gram precision of the translation against one or more human reference translations, penalizing translations that are too short. A BLEU of 30+ on a standard benchmark is considered reasonable; state-of-the-art systems reach 40–50+ on well-studied language pairs like English-German.

Low-resource translation (rare language pairs with little training data) is still a major challenge. Techniques include transfer learning from high-resource language pairs, multilingual models like mBART and NLLB-200, and back-translation to generate synthetic training data.

Why this matters in AI
Translation is the prototypical seq2seq task. Understanding its architecture explains how summarization, dialogue, and question answering models work.
Common mistakes
  • Using BLEU as the only metric — it correlates poorly with human judgement for fluency and fails on paraphrases.
  • Assuming high BLEU on newstext means the model works for your domain (medical, legal) — domain shift is severe.
  • Testing only on common language pairs — English-French may score 45 BLEU while the same model scores 12 on a low-resource language.

ROUGE BLEU perplexity

Detailed explanation

ROUGE (Recall-Oriented Understudy for Gisting Evaluation) measures the overlap between generated text and human reference text. ROUGE-1 counts matching unigrams, ROUGE-2 counts matching bigrams, ROUGE-L uses the longest common subsequence. It is the standard metric for summarization and is also used for translation and question answering.

BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) measures precision of n-grams in the generated text against reference translations, plus a brevity penalty for too-short outputs. It is the standard metric for translation. BLEU is computed as the geometric mean of 1-gram through 4-gram precisions multiplied by the brevity penalty. A BLEU of 0 means no n-gram overlap; 100 means perfect match.

Perplexity measures how well a language model predicts a test sequence. A lower perplexity means the model is less surprised by the text — it assigns high probability to the actual words. Perplexity of 1 means perfect prediction; higher values mean more uncertainty. GPT-2 achieves perplexity around 20–30 on standard benchmarks. Perplexity is used to compare language models but does not directly measure usefulness.

None of these metrics perfectly captures human quality. High ROUGE summaries can be incoherent; high BLEU translations can be unnatural; low perplexity models can still hallucinate. Always combine automatic metrics with human evaluation or task-based evaluation (does the chatbot help users accomplish their goal?).

Worked example

Follow the numeric walkthrough below. It starts with a tiny function or training situation, calculates the important quantity, and then connects that number back to model learning.

Reference summary: 'The president signed the bill into law on Friday.'
Generated: 'On Friday the president signed the new law.'
ROUGE-1: count shared unigrams — 7 of 9 generated words appear in reference → recall ≈ 0.78, precision ≈ 0.78.
ROUGE-2: shared bigrams — 'the president', 'signed the', 'the new/bill' — compute overlap.
BLEU: compute 1-gram through 4-gram precision with brevity penalty → single score between 0 and 100.
Why this matters in AI
You need automatic metrics to evaluate NLP models at scale. Understanding what they measure and what they miss helps you know when to trust them and when to do human evaluation.
Common mistakes
  • Using ROUGE or BLEU as the sole evaluation signal without reading model outputs — metrics reward n-gram overlap, not correctness.
  • Comparing BLEU scores across different tokenizers — scores are not comparable unless computed with the same tokenization.
  • Optimizing perplexity at the expense of factual accuracy — a model can have low perplexity while generating fluent hallucinations.
